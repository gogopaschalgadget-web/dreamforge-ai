The difference between a mediocre AI image and a stunning one almost always comes down to the prompt. Not the model. Not the settings. The prompt. The same image generator that produces generic, forgettable outputs for most users will produce gallery-quality work for someone who understands how to communicate with it.

This guide covers how to write AI art prompts that actually work in 2026, with specific techniques for Midjourney v7, plus free alternatives you can use without a subscription. Everything here is based on documented prompt structures and real examples, not theory.

How AI Art Prompts Work: The Mental Model You Need

AI image models do not "read" your prompt the way a human would. They match the words and phrases in your prompt to patterns learned during training. This means specificity almost always beats generality, and visual language beats conceptual language.

"A beautiful landscape" produces something. "A fog-covered mountain valley at dawn, golden hour light, mist in the foreground, watercolor painting style, soft edges, muted blues and greens" produces something specific. The second prompt gives the model enough signal to converge on what you actually want rather than averaging across all landscapes it has ever seen.

The core insight: every word in your prompt is a weight that pulls the output in a direction. Weak, vague words pull weakly. Specific, descriptive words pull hard. Use fewer words but make them count.

Prompt Structure: The Four-Part Framework

Every effective AI art prompt has four components. You do not always need all four, but knowing what each does helps you add the right pieces when your output is missing something.

Part 1: Subject

What is in the image? Be specific about the main subject, its action or pose, and any notable characteristics. "A woman reading" is weak. "A young woman in Victorian dress, sitting in a leather armchair, reading by firelight, glasses perched on her nose, absorbed in a thick leather-bound book" gives the model much more to work with.

Part 2: Setting and Atmosphere

Where is this happening? What does the environment look like? What is the mood? Atmosphere words like "melancholy", "serene", "tense", "ethereal" work well as mood anchors when combined with specific visual descriptions of the environment.

Part 3: Style Reference

What visual tradition does this belong to? Photography, oil painting, watercolor, digital illustration, concept art, woodblock print, studio photography, Polaroid, 35mm film, cinematic still. Naming a specific artist or style ("in the style of Art Deco illustration", "like a National Geographic photograph") gives the model a very precise target.

Part 4: Technical Parameters

Lighting, camera angle, depth of field, color palette. "Golden hour side lighting, shallow depth of field, shot from below" tells the model not just what to draw but how to draw it. In Midjourney, parameters like --ar 16:9 (aspect ratio), --chaos 20 (variation amount), and --style raw (less stylized output) go at the end after two hyphens.

Midjourney V7: What Changed in 2026

Midjourney V7, released in early 2026, made two significant changes to prompt behavior. First, it significantly improved its handling of multi-character scenes, which were notoriously difficult in previous versions. You can now describe two or three distinct characters in the same scene with much better accuracy. Second, it reduced the model's tendency to over-stylize. Earlier versions often produced images that looked like Midjourney images regardless of what style you asked for. V7 follows style instructions more faithfully.

What this means for your prompts: you can now be less heavy-handed with style language. V7 will follow a clean "studio product photography, white background, soft box lighting" prompt without you needing to add five additional style-enforcement words. Simpler prompts often outperform elaborate ones in V7.

The Most Common Prompt Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

Contradiction in the prompt: "A hyperrealistic oil painting" cannot exist because they are two different things. Pick one. If you want painterly with photorealistic detail, try "oil painting with hyperrealistic detail and texture" instead.

Too many competing subjects: "A woman, a cat, a mountain, a river, a sunset, and a village" forces the model to average everything. One primary subject with secondary elements that support it produces far stronger images.

Weak style language: "Beautiful" and "stunning" are nearly meaningless to image models. Replace them with visual specifics: "rich color palette", "dramatic chiaroscuro lighting", "razor-sharp detail in the foreground". Tell the model what beauty looks like in your image, not that it should exist.

Not specifying aspect ratio: The default square format is wrong for most uses. Use --ar 16:9 for landscape, --ar 9:16 for portrait (social media), --ar 4:5 for Instagram, --ar 3:2 for photography.

The 30-second test: Read your prompt aloud. If you cannot picture the image as you read it, the model will not be able to either. A good prompt is a painting in words. If it is vague to you, it will be vague in the output.
