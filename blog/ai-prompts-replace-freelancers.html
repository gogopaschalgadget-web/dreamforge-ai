Prompt 1: Product Description Writing

Freelance copywriters charge $500 to $2,000 per product description for e-commerce. You have 30 products. Do the math: that is $15,000 to $60,000 to get all your product pages written.

This is routine work. The AI is good at it. Use this prompt:

You are an expert e-commerce copywriter. Write a product description for: [product name and specs]. Target audience: [describe buyer]. Tone: [confident, friendly, technical, etc]. Include: key benefits (not features), pain point it solves, proof (if any), call to action. Format: 150 words max, SEO-optimized, no marketing fluff. Use short sentences.

Real example: A team with 40 products used this prompt and reduced description writing from 30 hours to 4 hours. Cost saved: $2,000 to $4,500 on freelance copywriting.

Human review still needed: 10 to 15 minutes per description to ensure accuracy and brand voice. But the baseline is generated, not blank.

Prompt 2: Customer Support Responses

Hiring a dedicated customer support person costs $3,000 to $6,000 per month. Training them takes weeks. They still make mistakes.

For routine customer questions, AI can handle the first draft. Your team does final review.

You are a customer support specialist. A customer wrote: [paste customer message]. Your company: [describe company and product]. Respond to their question/issue in one paragraph. Tone: empathetic, professional, solution-focused. Include: acknowledgment of issue, clear next step, contact info if escalation needed. Keep it under 100 words.

Real example: A SaaS company with 50 customer emails per day used this prompt and reduced response time from 2 hours to 10 minutes per email. One person now handles volume that used to require a full support team on routine questions.

Complex issues still need human judgment. But 70% of customer email is routine and now AI-assisted.

Prompt 3: Blog Post Outlines and First Drafts

Hiring a blog writer costs $100 to $300 per post, plus 2 to 4 weeks of back-and-forth revisions. Most teams outsource to cheaper writers and regret the quality.

AI can produce a first draft in minutes. Your team edits, adds proprietary insights, and publishes.

Write a blog post outline for: [topic]. Target reader: [describe audience]. Tone: [expert, conversational, instructional]. Sections: [5 to 7 sections that make sense]. Include: engaging intro hook, clear H2 subheadings, practical advice, CTA at the end. Format as outline with key points per section, not full prose.

After the outline is approved, generate the full post section by section, feeding back your edits to improve each one.

Real example: A software company using this workflow reduced blog production from 4 weeks per post to 1 week, with 2 of those days being human review and editing. Cost savings: $2,400+ per month on freelance writers.

Prompt 4: Email Campaign Templates

Email copywriting is specialized and expensive. Good email writers charge $2,000 to $5,000 per campaign. Bad campaigns leave revenue on the table.

AI is decent at email structure. You provide the product and audience, AI generates 3 to 5 versions, you pick the best one, you tweak it.

Write 3 subject lines and email body copy for: [campaign goal]. Product/offer: [describe]. Target audience: [describe]. Email length: [short/medium/long]. Tone: [casual, professional, urgent, etc]. Include: hook, benefit statement, social proof if applicable, clear CTA with link. Format: subject line first, then email body. Make it convert.

Real example: An e-commerce store used this prompt to generate email campaigns instead of hiring freelancers. Average open rate stayed the same (industry standard 20 to 25%), but save time was 20 hours per month per campaign.

Real copy editing is still needed (AI sometimes sounds corporate), but the structure and flow are there.

Prompt 5: Competitive Analysis and Research Synthesis

Research tasks are time-consuming and vague. You have 10 competitor websites, 5 industry reports, and scattered data. Someone needs to synthesize it into one clear summary. That usually falls to a team member and eats a full day.

Give that work to AI. Paste the raw material, ask specific questions.

Analyze these competitor websites and reports: [paste text]. Answer: 1) What are the top 3 competitive advantages they claim? 2) What gaps do they have? 3) What pricing model is most common? 4) What features are must-have vs. nice-to-have? Format as bullet points. Be specific and honest about where they win.

Real example: A B2B software company used this prompt to analyze 15 competitors in 30 minutes. Without AI, this task took a contractor 5 hours at $75/hour, costing $375. AI version: $0.50 in API costs.

Output requires human review (AI sometimes hallucinates), but it saves 90% of the manual work.

The Pattern: When AI Works and When It Does Not

These 5 prompts share a pattern: routine, high-volume work where consistency is more important than brilliance. AI excels here.

AI does NOT work for:

  • Brand voice that requires deep personality (hire a human who understands your brand)
  • Complex strategy that requires industry expertise (AI hallucinates stats and claims)
  • Legal or financial content (liability is too high)
  • Anything involving real customer data or secrets

AI DOES work for:

  • First drafts and structure (humans refine)
  • Routine customer service (humans escalate complex issues)
  • Data synthesis and summaries (humans verify facts)
  • Content variations (humans pick the best one)
  • Brainstorming and ideation (humans decide)
